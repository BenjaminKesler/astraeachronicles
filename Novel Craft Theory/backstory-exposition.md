# Backstory & Exposition
# source: "How To Write BACKSTORY + EXPOSITION The Right Way" — Abbiee — https://youtu.be/8F0d2Ma0-74

---

CORE REFRAME: backstory = exposition
  not two things where one is good and one is bad — same mechanism
  question is never "should I use exposition?" but "what kind, when, and how?"
  "avoid exposition at all costs" advice = accidentally advising against character investment

TWO-TIER THRESHOLD
  world-building/historical exposition: brain fills gaps readily; minimize freely
  character-emotional exposition: cannot be minimized — it IS the driving force
  diagnostic: is this telling the reader a fact about the world, or why they should care about a person?

THREE ESSENTIAL BACKSTORY ELEMENTS (Abbiee)
  what the character desires and believes will make them happy
  what the character is afraid of / worst-case scenario
  what the character mistakenly believes is true + the past event that caused that misbelief
  note: causal event is a distinct element — not just having a misbelief, but the wound that created it

INTROVERT TEST (diagnostic)
  if a reader could explain behavior as personality trait ("she's just an introvert")
  rather than as wound ("she was traumatized into isolation") → backstory is missing
  surface behavior without visible origin reads as character type, not character

---

DELIVERY PATHS (determined by character's dramatic function — not style preference)

WOUND-FIRST PATH
  open with dramatized backstory before present-tense story begins
  required when: character's dramatic irony depends on reader holding wound + present behavior simultaneously
  if reader doesn't hold both from the start, irony collapses into personality

MYSTERY-FIRST PATH
  establish puzzling present behavior first; deliver backstory mid-story (flashbacks)
  required when: character's appeal lives in the unknown — mystery requires a gap the reader notices
  opening with backstory pre-fills the gap before the reader knows to want it
  flashbacks can mirror the arc: backstory revealed as the character's own defenses drop

PATH SELECTION RULE
  wound-first for characters whose irony requires the reader to hold the gap from the start
  mystery-first for characters whose appeal is "who are you and why are you like this?"
  wrong path collapses the mechanism: wound-first kills mystery; mystery-first kills irony

---

DRAMATIZED VS. SUMMARIZED (the hook regression problem)
  problem: "give exposition so readers care about the character"
    → what makes readers care about the exposition itself? → infinite regression
  summary form cannot break the regression — it needs prior investment to land
  escape: backstory must contain its own dramatic event — wound occurs, not reported
  the backstory scene needs its own stakes and arc, even if brief
  diagnostic: "Does something actually happen inside my backstory, or am I reporting what happened?"
