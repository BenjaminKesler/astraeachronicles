# TYPE 7 — HEAD · forward-escape

WOUND: fear of being TRAPPED in pain (not fear of pain — fear of captivity in it with no exit). Continuous flight from deprivation/confinement to suffering. The lightness is real AND it is a system.
MECH [reframing]: primary cognitive tool and armor. Converts negatives into positives — setback = detour, loss = lesson. Often genuinely useful. Becomes pathological when the thing being reframed is heavy enough that the reframe is simply untrue. Engine activates when reframing stops working.
MECH [gluttony of options]: accumulates options and possibilities, not just experiences. Even PLANNING future pleasures is soothing (= escape routes). Doesn't just want experiences — wants to retain the *possibility* of having them. Commitment is threatening because it permanently removes possibilities from the menu.
MECH [commitment paradox]: fleeing captivity but building a life that is, in its emptiness, its own cage. Hakuna Matata phase is hollow. "The freedom they've built doesn't feel like freedom anymore."
ARC: stop running and face the dark. NOT a values change or new information — cessation of a behavior. Remaining present through whatever was behind the flight.
ENGINE: gap between performed ease and the reality being evaded. "Why are you running, and when will you stop?"
TENSION: commitment = captivity = forces contact with the dark thing they've spent their life avoiding. Even the seven who charges into danger (recklessness, bravado) is still running — toward stimulation, away from the dark thing.
KEY STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENT: escape mechanism must be EXHAUSTED, not just slowed. A seven who faces the dark because there's a brief pause in stimulation has not changed — they've paused. Story must progressively close off exits.
THE SPECIFIC DARK THING MATTERS: general avoidance = the type; the particular content of the avoidance = the character. Find it specifically (death, grief, guilt, responsibility, traumatic memory, the trail of destruction). The rest of the character follows from the specific dark thing.
STRESS→1: cornered, exits closed → suddenly rigid, moralistic, opinionated, critical. Perfectionism in one area while chaos persists everywhere else. Stress behavior, not character development.
INT→5: depth over breadth; willing to go deep on one thing; solitude without restlessness

SHAPES
  redeem: exhaust the exits → corner the character → they choose to stay (Puss in Boots: The Last Wish is most structurally explicit). Love story almost always interlocks — inability to commit to mortality = same inability to commit to the person. They're the same story.
  tragedy: doesn't face the dark → sinks further into mechanism until it consumes them (Better Call Saul). If clarity arrives just before the end, lean into it — the seven who sees clearly then is taken by consequences anyway is tragedy at its most complete.
  "treasure was home" shape: pursues the excess/novelty → finds it unsatisfying → what they were looking for was never out there. Not stopping the flight but discovering what the flight was actually for.

ANTAG: damage = what they fail to do, not what they do. Charming rogue who leaves wreckage. Not malicious — absent.
  charming defector (never commits, damage accumulates) | excess machine (Midas; gluttony pursued without limit becomes predatory) | rationalizer (reframing at antagonist scale; makes you believe the disaster is fine)
ANTAG weakness: force them to stay. The seven's power as antagonist dissolves when they can no longer leave. Cannot be argued out of running — close the exits.

NOT
  → sunshine caricature is the single biggest failure mode — find the panic behind the eyes. "It's fine, everything's great" — the panic is there. Write it as visible to reader even when invisible to other characters.
  → sevens CAN be dark — the escape can look like war, addiction, rebellion, recklessness. Surface can be almost anything; what matters is the arrangement: painful reality underneath, avoidance behavior on top.
  → moralism trap: resist writing "I didn't know it was wrong, now I do." The arc that works: "I knew, and I ran from it anyway, and now I can't run anymore." The fixation is an active strategy, not ignorance. The character must own the role they played in their own corruption.
  → type can manifest at thematic/structural level, not just individual character — a society or culture refusing to face unavoidable reality can be seven-coded. Structural possibilities beyond the individual.

DX
  1. What is this character running from specifically — the particular painful reality/memory/emotion/consequence the escape was built to avoid?
  2. What is their escape mechanism — social (adventure, novelty), intellectual (constant new projects), aggressive (recklessness)? What does it look like to an outside observer who doesn't yet know it's avoidance?
  3. What does the Hakuna Matata phase look like — how long has it been operational? What damage trail have they left, and who has paid the cost?
  4. What is their reframing mechanism — how do they convert the dark thing? Where does it visibly fail, where does the reframe not quite hold?
  5. What is their relationship to commitment — what specific form does captivity take, and what is their exit strategy when relationships demand sustained presence?
  6. What is the backstory — what trap did they escape from, and what did they decide (below articulation) would never happen again?
  7. What closes the exits — what mechanism in the plot removes the escape routes and forces presence? (Most important structural question; story cannot complete without this answer.)
  8. What does the dark thing actually look like when they can no longer run from it — how bad is it actually vs. how bad the seven has been treating it as?
  9. What does romantic commitment cost this character specifically — what hard conversation, what sustained presence do they most resist?
  10. When this seven goes into one-stress — cornered, exits closed — what does the rigidity look like, and who does the moralism get directed at first?
  11. What would integration look like as a behavior on the page — what one thing might they commit to fully, what would they discover about inner life if they stayed still?
  12. What is the specific cost of their avoidance the story must make visible — what particular person/relationship/outcome is the direct consequence, and who pays the price?
