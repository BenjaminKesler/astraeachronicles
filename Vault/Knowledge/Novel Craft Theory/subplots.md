# Subplots
# source: "HOW TO DO SUBPLOTS - THE RIGHT WAY" — Abbiee (Writer's Life Wednesdays) — https://youtu.be/Whs7SRPZ0NY

---

CRAI METHOD (subplot tracking tool)
  per subplot, document four slots:
  CHARACTER: who is this person
  ROLE: their relationship to the protagonist — the basis of reader investment in them
  AGENDA: what the subplot character wants
  IMPACT: how they affect the main conflict or main character
  diagnostic: can't fill Impact → subplot isn't load-bearing yet; keep developing

ANTICIPATION MECHANIC (reader engagement engine)
  readers stay with subplots not from intrinsic interest in subplot characters
  but because they're running a background thread: "what happens when the protagonist finds out?"
  reader holds the protagonist in mind while watching the subplot — waiting for collision
  → this is WHY protagonist connection is required, not just that it is
  villain exception: writers apply this logic instinctively to the villain subplot and stop
    other subplots drift without a collision point — this is where subplot interest dies

PROTAGONIST AS YARDSTICK
  everything in a story derives importance from how important it is to the protagonist
  subplot offscreen from the protagonist still holds reader interest via the anticipation mechanic
  without a visible collision point: reader brain has no thread to hold

FLOOR / BONUS FRAMEWORK
  FLOOR (required): relational tether — subplot character connects to someone reader already cares about
  BONUS: thematic resonance — subplot echoes or contrasts the central question
  authority note: for the primary B-story subplot, thematic resonance is structural not optional (Snyder, Weiland, Truby)
    Snyder: B-story's primary function is theme delivered through relationship — relational is vehicle, not destination
    lighter thematic touch is defensible for secondary subplots in a thematically dense ensemble
  ensemble corollary: when protagonists already saturate the thematic space at full weight
    side characters should echo one protagonist's thematic angle, not introduce a new one
    adding another angle dilutes rather than reinforces

DIVIDED-POV DIAGNOSTIC (multi-protagonist intro)
  # source: "ADDING/SWITCHING PROTAGONISTS IN A STORY" — Abbiee — https://youtu.be/8qUgH7OOBBs
  counter-stereotype: a POV opened WITHOUT relational context divides investment — thins BOTH characters, not just the new one (earlier ≠ more bonded)
  trip-wire: the FELT sense of being pulled too many directions early — not a checklist
  test: at the moment each POV opens, does the reader already hold a thread that makes this person matter?
  remedy menu (not a single fix):
    relational link — tie new POV to an established character; cheapest, but overuse breeds contrivance at ensemble scale (small-world feel)
    theme-as-yardstick — POV that's a clear facet of the central question isn't cold even with no character link (scales to large casts) → see below
    converging event — one inciting catastrophe all are caught in gives every POV a held thread independent of who's met whom

ENSEMBLE YARDSTICK EXTENSION
  single-protagonist: protagonist IS the yardstick
  true ensemble: central thematic question can serve as yardstick — ONLY if all protagonists are genuine facets of that question
    protagonists as N angles on the same question → subplot can tether to any protagonist and feel relevant
    protagonists as N separate questions sharing a setting → investment is fragmented; subplot anchor is lost
  authority note: McKee argues there's always one central protagonist even in ensemble structures
    Weiland and Truby support theme-as-yardstick for multi-protagonist work
  diagnostic: can I state the central thematic question tightly enough to filter subplot characters against it?
