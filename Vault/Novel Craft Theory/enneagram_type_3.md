# TYPE 3 — HEART · performing-to-prove

WOUND: trying to please the void. Love = prize awarded for performance. NOT ambition — survival. No superiority complex, only inferiority compensated for. Write from the terrified kid inward.
MECH [goalpost moves]: external success cannot resolve the wound — the wound was never about the achievement. No matter how high they climb, they will never be satisfied. A three story that ends with them winning the prize ≠ a three story.
MECH [comparative imperative]: fixation is always comparative. Not "I want to succeed" but "succeed where the people who doubted me could see it." Ask: who specifically are they trying to outshine? There's almost always a specific reference group embedded in the goal.
ARC: stop acting like the thing and be the thing. Persona must collapse under the weight of the actual person inside it.
ENGINE: obsession story — most devastating moments often have nothing to do with the goal (how relentlessly they abandoned the people who mattered)
TENSION: deepest fear ≠ failure — it's being a fraud. A victory they didn't earn doesn't fill the hole; it just reminds them they're a liar.
STRESS→9: achievement machine breaks → apathy, numbness, disengagement — the relentlessly driven character suddenly cannot find a single thing to care about
INT→6: letting go of individual glory → worth in belonging to a community rather than standing above one; stepping down from center

SHAPES
  redeem: must fail in some meaningful way; getting everything they ever wanted CAN itself be failure (A Star is Born — success-as-erasure)
  tragedy: persona wins. Never exposed → lifetime winning prizes for a person they never were. Or exposed (Stringer Bell).
  ULTIMATE (never yet depicted in fiction): permanently ruins reputation for greater good and never tells anyone. Never spins it. Takes it to the grave. The only action structurally immune to the "did I do it for praise?" loop — you cannot be applauded for something no one knows about.

ANTAG: authority requires an audience — crumbles against a gut-type who acts from pure conviction without needing anyone's approval. Love-hungry, not power-hungry — power is just the best vehicle found. Even the darkest three villain would fold if someone told them they were enough.
Low-health texture: robotic efficiency — steamrolls not from hatred but because "it's good business." Feelings aren't gone, they're buried. If you crack the armor with unexpected kindness, the machine stutters.

NOT
  → fixation doesn't apply uniformly — a three boxer may have no interest in how they dress; all three energy goes into the ring. Type = the why; the specific domain is the character.
  → CAN channel fixation toward moral causes — still a three, motivation is still comparative; tell: compliment them and they giggle
  → "entitled finance bro" caricature obscures the wound — always write from the shame backward

DX
  1. What specific achievement has self-worth attached to — what does "success" look like in concrete terms?
  2. Who is the reference group — who specifically are they trying to outshine, impress, or prove wrong?
  3. Where were they shot in the leg at the starting line — the origin disadvantage that started the engine?
  4. What persona have they built over the wound — how does it differ from who they actually are underneath?
  5. When the goalpost moves — success doesn't satisfy — what does this three do next? Double down, pivot, or crack?
  6. What relationship will refuse to accept the persona and reach for the real person?
  7. Where in the story does the three confuse persona with actual self — where does the mask slip without them realizing?
  8. What would it cost to permanently ruin their reputation for the greater good — could they do it and never tell anyone?
  9. Does the romantic subplot feed the self-esteem machine (parallel goal) or function as genuine counterforce demanding authenticity?
  10. Low health: do they go robotic — feelings suppressed, forward motion at all costs? Or stress toward nine-like apathy and shutdown?
  11. What does the inner child believe — not the adult, the child who formed the wound?
  12. What would stepping down from the center and becoming part of a community look like — how far is that from where they start?
