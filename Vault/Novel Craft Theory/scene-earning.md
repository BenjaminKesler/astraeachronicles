# Scene Earning & Planted Resonance
# source: "Why Your Most Important Scenes Don't Work" — Melissa Bourbon — https://youtu.be/jo29IY2TAMY

MISDIAGNOSIS TRAP
  instinct when a scene falls flat: revise the scene (dialogue, emotion, stakes)
  almost always wrong — the problem is 50–100 pages earlier, not inside the scene
  diagnostic signal: readers describe a major scene as "fine" → setup deficit, not scene deficit
  fix direction: look upstream, not inside

PAYOFF REDEFINITION
  payoff ≠ the moment itself
  payoff = relationship between the moment and everything that came before it
  implication: cannot fix a flat payoff by strengthening the scene — only by strengthening the approach

SHARED NOSTALGIA (the emotional mechanism)
  readers who "live through" events alongside a character develop retrospective attachment
  payoff moment becomes a portal — reader simultaneously feels current scene + all accumulated prior moments
  distinct from investment (stakes-based) — nostalgia is about shared memory, not just what matters
  small specific details (drumming on every surface) carry more nostalgic weight than large plot events

PLANTED RESONANCE (the technique)
  details that look like texture on first read; function as emotional anchors at payoff
  not foreshadowing (signals future plot) — reader isn't meant to notice the setup
  not random texture — every planted detail is load-bearing for a specific future payoff
  naming them separately from "texture" prevents accidental clustering or randomness

REVERSE-ENGINEER PROTOCOL
  1. identify the intended emotional payoff (not the plot beat — the feeling)
  2. ask: what does the reader need to have "shared" with this character for this to hit?
  3. identify specific small moments that would create that shared nostalgic texture
  4. distribute those moments across the full arc — never cluster

DISTRIBUTION PRINCIPLE
  nostalgic payoff requires pattern, not block — reader needs to feel accumulation across the whole story
  front-loading creates setup deficit in Act 2/3; back-loading means the pool isn't full yet
  never dump a concentration of planted moments in one scene and leave others bare
  goal: reader has been collecting planted moments the whole way through without knowing it

PREPARE ≠ PREDICT
  setup ≠ foreshadowing/telegraphing future events
  setup = building emotional substrate so readers have something at stake when the moment arrives
  predicting tells readers what will happen; preparing makes them care when it does

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS
  "What has my story done to earn this moment?"
  "What does the reader need to have 'shared' with this character for this payoff to feel nostalgic?"
  "Are my planted moments distributed across the arc or clustered?"
  "Am I trying to fix the scene, or am I looking upstream where the real problem is?"
