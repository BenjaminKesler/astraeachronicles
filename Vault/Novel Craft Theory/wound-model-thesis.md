# The Wound Model — Unified Craft Theory
# synthesized thesis — session work, 2026-06-20
# cross-references: [[internal-conflict]], [[wound-protective-mechanisms]], [[backstory-exposition]], [[plot-structure-outlining]]

## Part 1: Formation

Every character arc begins with a wound — a formative experience of genuine threat, loss, humiliation, or destabilization. The wound is an event, not yet a belief. What matters is not the event itself but what the mind does with it in the immediate aftermath.

The wound produces two things simultaneously, not sequentially. The first is **fear**: a threat-pattern encoding — *this type of experience is dangerous*. The second is **abstract desire**: the equal and opposite pull — *I need whatever would make this safe*. Fear and abstract desire are co-produced, two poles of the same wound response. You cannot have one without the other. This is why Enneagram types always pair a core fear with a core desire: they're not two separate facts about a character, they're one wound seen from two directions.

While still in the acute state — exposed, afraid, desperate to make the pain stop — the character's mind narrativizes. It constructs a cognitive worldview to explain what happened and protect against recurrence. This is the **misbelief**: *this is how the world works*. The misbelief forms under pressure, which means it forms badly. It's a hasty interpretation that crystallizes into a fixed rule before the character has enough distance to evaluate it clearly.

The misbelief does two jobs simultaneously, and this dual function is the most important structural insight in the model. First, it **manages the fear** — it makes the threat feel rational and, crucially, controllable. If *this is how the world works*, then *doing X will keep me safe*. The fear stops feeling like chaos and starts feeling like a problem the character can manage. Second, it acts as a **translation layer** — it converts the abstract desire into a specific, concrete, actionable goal. *I need to be loved unconditionally* (abstract) becomes *I need to make myself indispensable so no one will leave* (surface). The surface desire feels inevitable because it grew from the wound's own logic. But it's pre-loaded with a fatal flaw: it is structured so that even perfect success cannot address the wound. It's a wrong solution not because it's a bad plan, but because it was designed to avoid the wound, not heal it.

Once formed, the system is **self-sealing**. Fear makes the misbelief feel necessary. The misbelief makes the fear feel rational. They continuously reinforce each other. The chain — wound, fear, misbelief, surface desire — traces the historical origin of the system. In the character's present, the system doesn't fire sequentially. It activates as a whole.

## Part 2: Maintenance

The character doesn't carry the misbelief passively. They actively maintain it through behavior. There are three distinct wound-protective mechanisms, and understanding all three is essential because they produce different surfaces on the page.

**Avoidance** — the character routes around any situation that would challenge the misbelief or expose the wound. On the page this reads as retreat, refusal, and detour. The reader's experience is tension: the thing they're running from is getting closer.

**Surrender** — the character actively runs toward the misbelief as a refuge, repeatedly re-choosing it when threatened. The misbelief isn't just a defensive wall; it's an active source of psychological safety that truth cannot yet offer. The character runs toward it as much as away from the wound. On the page this reads as self-sabotage and wrong-refuge-seeking. The reader's experience is empathetic frustration: you can see it isn't working.

**Overcompensation** — the character performs the exact opposite of their wound's content to prove the wound doesn't exist. Someone who fears weakness becomes hypercompetent and domineering. Someone who fears abandonment becomes fiercely self-sufficient. On the page this reads as apparent strength or mastery — the most deceptive mechanism because it can fool the reader into thinking the character has their wound handled. The reader's experience is creeping dread as the armor visibly strains.

All three mechanisms serve the same function: protecting the misbelief from challenge. Each character has a **dominant mechanism** — a primary wound-protective style that defines how they move through the world and constitutes part of their character signature.

At the scene level, however, the mechanism that activates is **context-sensitive**. Three variables determine which mechanism fires in any given scene: the relationship (who is present and what the power dynamic is — a character may overcompensate with strangers and surrender with intimates), the threat level (low threat may not activate the wound at all; extreme threat can overwhelm the dominant mechanism and force a backup one the character is far less practiced at), and the **arc position** (early in the story the dominant mechanism is intact and well-practiced; as the story applies pressure it becomes increasingly costly to maintain; at the disaster it breaks).

Mechanism shifts across the arc are **diagnostic signals** of arc progression. If a character who primarily overcompensates starts avoiding in Act 2, that's not inconsistency — that's the armor cracking. If the character is doing exactly the same thing in Act 2 that they did in Act 1, the arc isn't moving.

Finally, **mechanism mismatch between characters in a scene generates specific friction**. An overcompensator paired with an avoider creates a pursuit dynamic. Two overcompensators create a dominance collision. A surrenderer paired with an avoider creates an approach-withdrawal loop. Character psychology doesn't just describe interior states — it predicts interpersonal scene dynamics.

## Part 3: The Story Engine

The surface desire and fear-avoidance are not two separate things running in parallel. They are the same behavior viewed from two angles.

The character cannot consciously pursue *avoid my wound* — that's not an actionable goal. So the misbelief converts the avoidance impulse into something that looks like a positive goal: *I want this thing*. The character genuinely believes pursuing it will bring fulfillment. What they cannot see is that the surface desire was selected — by the wound's own logic — specifically because chasing it doesn't require confronting the wound. The surface desire is fear-avoidance wearing a costume.

This means the story is running two games simultaneously for the reader. The first game: will the character get the thing? This is the surface tension. The second game: can they keep routing around the wound while trying to get it? This is the deeper tension, and it's more interesting — because it's the game the character doesn't know they're playing.

What makes Act 2 structurally coherent is that the character could theoretically solve their problems if they were willing to confront the wound. They won't. The misbelief tells them that confronting it would be worse than any obstacle. So they find wound-safe workarounds. The plan grows increasingly precarious because the one move that would actually work is always the move they won't make. The story is a compression chamber: each scene exhausts another avoidance route, until none remain.

## Part 4: The Arc

**The disaster** must hit the misbelief specifically — not just the surface desire. This is the critical requirement. A disaster that damages the surface desire but leaves the misbelief intact produces biochemical response in the reader — they feel the tension — but doesn't produce identification, which requires inhabiting the character's psychology. The disaster that works does three things: it is **self-caused** (every wound-driven decision has accumulated causally to this moment, and the reader has had a front-row seat to the hole being dug); it **strips the dominant mechanism** (the armor cracks, the safety net fails, the avoidance route closes — whichever mechanism was primary is specifically what breaks); and it **exposes the raw wound directly**, with all protective routing removed.

The specific shape of the disaster must be calibrated to the character's wound and dominant mechanism, not borrowed from a generic disaster template.

**The dark moment** follows. The character is broken, without the belief system that has organized their entire life. The dominant mechanism is gone. This is not an externally inflicted punishment — it is the floor of the hole they dug themselves, which is the only reason it can lead anywhere productive. The dark moment is the required precondition for the aha moment, because revelation cannot land while the misbelief is still providing cover.

**The aha moment** is not the delivery of new information. The abstract desire has existed since the wound's formation — it's been there all along, one of the two things the wound produced. The misbelief was obscuring it. What dissolves in the aha moment is the cognitive worldview that was making the fear feel rational and necessary. The fear itself may remain as a felt response, but it no longer controls behavior, because the structure that was amplifying and managing it is gone. For the first time, the character can see what they have actually needed all along.

**The climax** tests whether the aha moment is real. The character must act from the dissolved-misbelief state under external pressure. If the internal transformation is genuine, it will hold when the story demands it. The climax is where internal and external become the same thing.

**Resolution splits into two paths.**

In a **positive arc**, the character acts from truth. The wound's cycle of suffering ends — not because the fear disappears, but because the misbelief that was amplifying it and routing all behavior through it is gone. The abstract desire can now be pursued honestly rather than through the wrong-solution surface desire.

In a **negative arc**, the character actively chooses the misbelief as the truth. This is not passive failure or tragic victimhood — it is an act of will. The character convinces themselves that the lie is the truth. The misbelief fully seals. The distinction between descending into darkness and committing to it is the line between a tragedy and a negative arc: one is something that happens to the character; the other is something the character does.

## Part 5: Cross-Cutting Properties

**The system is circular, not linear.** The chain traces historical origins. In the character's present, fear and misbelief reinforce each other continuously. Writing about a character in a scene doesn't mean moving them through the chain in sequence — it means working with a fully integrated, self-sealing system that activates as a whole when the wound is triggered.

**The misbelief functions as active refuge, not just defensive wall.** The character doesn't merely fail to challenge the misbelief — they return to it. Every time a scene creates a situation that could expose the wound, the character routes around (avoidance), runs toward the familiar wrong comfort (surrender), or performs invulnerability (overcompensation). All three are active, not passive. The misbelief provides safety that the truth cannot yet offer. This is why dissolution is structurally hard: the character is being asked to give up their safety net before a replacement exists.

**Abstract desire is present from the wound but legible only at the aha moment.** It does not arrive at the resolution — it surfaces. The character isn't receiving something new; they're finally seeing something that was always true about themselves, that the misbelief had been covering.

**The dominant mechanism is a character signature; scene-level activation is context-sensitive.** Mechanism shifts signal arc movement. Mechanism mismatch generates scene friction. These are tools for both character design and scene construction.
